Mansfield Park

About

The role and impact of Literature throughout history has always been large as literature has been present in all types of civilisations through art, songs, poetry, storytelling, visual and musical arts, etc.

The aim of this website it to create a unique and simple explanation of the historical relationship between Empire and Literature.

Our goals are to enlighten, educate and pique the interest of viewers/users on how literature contributed to the expansion of colonialism through the historical relationship between Empire and Literature. We achieved this goal by looking at Jane Austen's novel Mansfield Park and how the novel can be linked to the relationship and expansion.

History of Empire and Literature Relationship Linked to Said critique

In his critique Said acknowledges the importance of the relationship between Empires and Literature through his definition of the relationship between Imperial institutions and culture in chapter 1 his book/work of criticism. Whilst defining the relationship between imperial institutions and culture. Said uses Reynold's discourses "Art and Science. Remove them or Degrade them and the Empire in No More." (Said, 46) to highlight that science was not the main foundation for the formation of Empires, that art, (which literature falls under), was also part of the foundation and played a large part in its formation.

Literature not only contributed to the formation of European but also. it's cultural formations. This can be seen in the first excerpt on page 46, in which Said states: "by the authority of recognizable cultural formations, by continuing consolidation within education, literature, and the visual and musical arts- were manifested at another very significant level, that of the national culture," (Said, 46). The excerpt quote not only confirms its role in cultural formations but also how deeply literature is embedded in the history of Empires, as it was not only one of the foundations but also contributed to the expansion of European colonial Empires. Therefore, the relationship between Empire and Literature was important to Europe as it was the foundation for most of its prominent Empirical formations and expansions.

Ties to Jane Austen's Mansfield Park

It is well known that Said dislikes the novel Mansfield Park, as he believes that Jane Austen supported the slavery and the anti- abolitionist movement. He critiques the novel to strengthen his argument. However, the novel can be linked to Said's critique of how literature and novels hold a connection between fiction and the historical world, as well as how this connection should be considered more valuable that art as it is affiliated with real world colonial settings (Said, 46). Austen who lived during the beginning and peak of the expansion uses her novel Mansfield Park to discuss certain topics surrounding the colonial trade and expansions especially in Chapters 3 and 21 in which her characters discuss Antigua which was one of the largest established sugar-based plantations and the slave trade.

Austen uses her literary profession to discuss the taboo topics of slavery and the abolitionist movement. By doing this she not only goes against Said's critique of her novel but also strengthen his definition and the relationship between Empire and Literature. Austen's discussion of these topics is done in subtle as to not draw too much attention to it. The discussion of Antigua which occurs in chapter 3 is the main tie to the relationship between Empire and Literature as it is discusses byAusten's character Lady Bertram and Mrs. Norris in a grave and controversial manner as Sir Thomas and the Bertrams wealth/income to the colonial trade, as stated in the previous the historical role of Antigua. Therefore, the novel Mansfield Park can be linked to the relationship between the Empire and Literature due to Austen's discussion of certain topics suck as Antigua within her novel.

Contribution to Colonialism

Literature was used by Empires to determine whether or not an individual was civilised or uncivilised, as being literate was a sign of both proper and civilisation and a progress marker of the expansion of colonialism and the Christian faith because many natives were forced to separate themselves or cut ties from their tribal/native roots and adopt the colonial religion, language and naming system. IN the second excerpt on page 48 Said strengthens the notion/discussion of literature contributing to Europe's colonial expansion when he states: "These habits seem to be guided by a powerful if imprecise notion that works of literature are autonomous......, the literature itself makes constant references to itself as somehow participating in Europe's overseas expansion.." (Said, 46). By stating this Said confirms the discussion/notion that literature contributed to the Europe's expansion.

However, it was not only done through novels, but through the colonial education system, and visual and musical arts. This is because Europe did not rely on one form literature as the natives within their other colonies used forms of literature such as storytelling or visual and musical arts to communicate and express themselves to one another. Meaning that Europe's expansion could not rely on traditional literature such as novels or poetry to endure its imperialistic empire control. Therefore, Literature's contribution to the expansion of colonialism played a key role as colonizers used different form of Literature to categorize and colonize the natives within their colonies.
